Your career beyond senior

January 09, 2023

The more we work on something, the better we get. Some people claim one needs to invest 10.000 hours into something to become and expert. Some say, 1.000 hours would be sufficient. However, no one would argue that the more you work on something, the deeper your knowledge becomes.

Out of the group of people who are working on a certain area, over time some would switch to a different area. Out of 10 backend engineers starting fresh out of college, may be 6 would continue working in this area for the next 3 years. 2 would switch to DevOps, 1 would switch to frontend and 1 would switch to management. I am showing a simplified, average case. If you take a longer period of time, the chances that people would switch to a different area would increase. This creates a funnel effect. It is always harder to find people with more experience in a specific area if you look for someone who is more experienced.

Everyone is looking to be recognized for our work and our continuous effort. More knowledgeble person also usually bringing more value to the company: they have a high-level understanding, domain-specific knowledge, communication skills. So we are looking to get promoted over time. As I and many other engineers progres in my career, we found ourselves in the situation where in order to get promoted, we have to start doing something that we have no experience with: management.

Does this make sense? I would say, not really, but this is the reality for many engineers who reached Senior level. There are more companies that have a smaller engineering department (take startups, agencies, etc.) than the big ones. In a small team, there is no room to promote an individual conributor above the senior level. Industry's mindset is stuck with Junior-Mid-Senior Engineer-Engineerin Manager promotion ladder.
